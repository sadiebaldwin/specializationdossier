Resources

Advice + annotated bibliography for classmates exploring emerging technologies

While this collection of resources serves as a beginning reference point for emerging LIS professionals and MLIS students interested in the world of emerging technologies, it is still a broad and complicated field of study and work. Edwin Rodarte at LAPL gives the following words of advice to anyone interested in this work.

Words of Advice

  • Take technology classes!
  • Strive to find tech courses, seminars, conferences, and programs both within and beyond your MLIS degree.
  • Learn different coding languages.
  • He took an HTML course in addition to his program at SJSU.
  • Advocate for the creation of specialized positions in your institution.
  • As we’ve explored, positions such as Edwin’s at LAPL are not standardized and often do not exist in libraries, despite their necessity in a growing digital world.
  • Acquire project management skills (grant writing, data analysis, Excel).
  • Emerging Technologies professionals should not just be well versed in the technology tools which they interact with and educate on, but in managerial and communication-based skills, too!
  • Keep the community needs first.
  • Edwin cites different tech educational engagement programs at LAPL which surprisingly failed. Programs that focused on Virtual Reality, for instance, were not met with excitement by the public they served, and so LAPL technology professionals like Edwin listened and pivoted their offerings (which impacted everything from scheduling to budgeting to communication and outreach!). Keeping the needs of your users at the forefront is essential for GLAM professions.

Annotated Bibliography

Below you’ll find a small collection of extra resources related to the growing field of emerging technologies in LIS professions.

Gilardian, Colby. “How Los Angeles Libraries Are Reinventing Themselves for Gen Z through Technology.” Annenberg Media, November 20, 2025. https://www.uscannenbergmedia.com/2025/11/20/how-los-angeles-libraries-are-reinventing-themselves-for-gen-z-through-technology/ .

This article discusses how USC and the Los Angeles Public Library are reshaping their libraries to include makerspaces and digital tools for young learners. The center of this is LAPL’s Octavia Lab, a full makerspace located in the notable Central Library in Downtown LA. This lab features 3D printers, a laser cutter, a media production studio, an embroidery machine, sewing machines, a photography space, vinyl and poster printing, and a full podcast studio. Free for those with a library card, community members use the space for professional portfolios, school projects, small-business production, or just a personal creative project. Gilardian discusses similar technology accessibility at the Science and Engineering Library at USC, through the Baum Family Maker Space. Students can develop prototypes, learn 3D modeling, participate in design workshops, and use fabrication tools. This is part of a campus-wide shift toward experiential learning. While our interview with Edwin offered an internal perspective on the labor, planning, and digital inclusion behind these services, this article helps show how the same developments appear from the user side as part of a larger effort to make library spaces more accessible and engaging for contemporary users. For LIS students, this source is useful because it connects abstract professional trends to a real public library and university library system, making the stakes of technology adoption visible and more concrete.

Radniecki, Tara. “Study on Emerging Technologies Librarians: How a New Library Position and Its Competencies Are Evolving to Meet the Technology and Information Needs of Libraries and Their Patrons.” 2013. https://repository.ifla.org/handle/20.500.14598/5094 .

This 2013 study deals with the evolving jobs and duties in the MLIS field for professionals to meet technological needs of their institutions and patrons. Although the publication date is more than a decade old, and thus does not take AI into account, the thesis of the study remains relevant: that libraries need to leverage and update new technologies constantly. At the time of publication, the author, Tara Radniecki, worked in the reference department at Loyola Marymount University; she is now the Associate Dean for the University of Arizona Libraries. The University of Arizona happens to be a pioneer in online learning, and thus Radniecki’s career move speaks to both her deep interest and expertise in matters relating to emerging technologies. The study asks four central questions: 1) What type of libraries are hiring emerging technologies librarians? 2) What are the anticipated responsibilities and required skills upon hire? 3) Do current emerging technologies librarians feel they have the skills necessary to complete all responsibilities? 4) Are current emerging technologies librarians satisfied in their current role? This tightly organized paper offers many illustrative answers while also leaving open new questions. Interestingly, according to the paper, no studies have analyzed job advertisements for emerging technologies librarians. The paper traces the advent of web-related librarian positions to 1990, providing a fascinating historical context to the current phenomenon of AI. That is how fast the profession has moved, and it only continues to move faster. Who knows where it will be in another thirty-five years?

Syme, Fiona. “The Impact of Emerging Technologies on Libraries.” collectionHQ, September 25, 2024. https://www.collectionhq.com/the-impact-of-emerging-technologies-on-libraries/ .

CollectionHQ, which published this blog post in 2024, was founded in the United Kingdom in 2008 to deliver data-driven action plans to empower librarians. Thus, the company has been a leading provider of new technologies in public library spaces, lending legitimacy to this publishing platform. The post offers a brief overview of some of these new technologies, including AI and VR, how they are implemented in libraries, and what impact they have on staff and patrons. The post is written for librarians and library professionals to prepare for continued trends and evolutions. While the post is fairly recent, which makes it a more valuable source, it has no author, which detracts from its authority. AI is explicitly discussed, however, which is instrumental for the purposes of our dossier. The style of the post is informal (there is even the use of an exclamation point!), which makes it more accessible to a wider readership. There are statistics from the American Library Association, supporting the post’s claims. The post concludes by assuring the reader that digital tools will never replace human librarians. The post emphasizes focusing on tools that improve librarians’ work and patrons’ experiences over time—an articulation of a central thesis within emerging technologies librarianship.

Tanzi, Nick. “Library Tech Trends for 2025.” The Digital Librarian, January 21, 2025. https://the-digital-librarian.com/2025/01/21/library-tech-trends-for-2025/ .

This is a catch-all brief look into rising tech trends in library professions. In this article, author Nick Tanzi deals with the growing number of ebook and e-audiobooks in library collections. This fact is directly related to the concerns of Emerging Technologies Librarians, who are responsible for equipping and informing their institutions as to rapidly changing developments in the field. Tanzi is well-situated to make these arguments, as he is an internationally recognized library technology consultant and author of the book Best Technologies for Public Libraries. The article also mentions legislative attempts to curb exploitative practices that raise costs of technologies for libraries, as well as “AI-enhanced” services. Friction between librarians and generative AI is also acknowledged as a complication of the current information environment. The article emphasizes computer literacy and methods of safety to guard against the potential dangers of generative and conversational AI. Given that Tanzi’s article appeared in 2025, this source is valuable as an up-to-date summary of both developments and concerns in the field.

Yang, Sharon Q., and Lili Li. Emerging Technologies for Librarians: A Practical Approach in Innovation. Kidlington, UK: Chandos Publishing, 2016. https://www.sciencedirect.com/book/monograph/9781843347880/emerging-technologies-for-librarians .

This book is useful as a foundational resource because it defines “emerging technologies” in a library context through their use in real situations. Yang and Li organize their work through a division of library functions, including library systems, discovery services, mobile apps, reference, metadata management, library instruction, research support, and more. Each chapter begins with a description of a specific emerging technology and its use in libraries, followed by a list of existing applications for that emerging technology. Because “emerging technologies” is so broad, it is impossible to cover every single technology, and this book doesn’t attempt to do that. It instead focuses on computer-related tools, including software, hardware, and cloud-based solutions. The authors present this book as a reference guide for any librarian who wants to know what applications are available for a new technology and what level of expertise will be needed to implement the technology. Although it predates the current ubiquity of AI, it still provides a strong baseline for understanding how library workers think about technological change and implementation in an institutional context. For LIS students, this is a helpful starting point because it introduces the broad scope of technology work in libraries and shows how new technology can be quickly integrated into everyday service.
